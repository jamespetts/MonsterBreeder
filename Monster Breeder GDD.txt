Monster Breeder - Concise Game Design Document

Document rule: this GDD describes the finished project design, not the implementation status, work history, or development sequence. Future updates must not add notes about what has already been implemented, what is intended next, what should be implemented before or after another feature, or any other project-log or roadmap material.

1. High Concept
Monster Breeder is a semi-turn-based monster-breeding defence game. The player breeds a genetically evolving population of autonomous monsters and deploys them to defend procedurally generated treasure sites from human adventurers.

The player's main control is artificial selection: choosing which monsters to breed, which to deploy, which to hold in reserve, and which wild monsters to capture to broaden the gene pool. During raids, monsters act autonomously according to inherited traits and immediate circumstances.

The player loses the campaign if the remaining treasure value after food expenditure is zero.

2. Core Fantasy
The player is the keeper of a monster brood defending treasure from greedy humans. Over many rounds, the player develops bloodlines of monsters suited to different terrains, treasure layouts and adventurer threats.

The game should feel like Creatures meets reverse tower defence / Dungeon Keeper, with genetics as the long-term strategy layer.

3. Overall Structure
The game proceeds in repeated rounds.

Each round has three broad phases.

3.1 Management/Breeding Phase
The player may:
- inspect surviving monsters;
- breed monsters;
- manage eggs and hatchlings;
- choose which monsters to release into the landscape;
- hold monsters in reserve;
- import/export monsters if desired;
- proceed when ready.

3.2 Real-Time Raid Phase
Adventurers enter the landscape. Adventurers try to steal treasure and escape. Released monsters roam autonomously and attack humans.

Player intervention is deliberately limited:
- deploy reserve monsters;
- recall deployed monsters to base;
- click wild monsters to capture them for future rounds.

There is no detailed RTS-style command structure.

3.3 Aftermath
The game calculates stolen and remaining treasure. Injuries, deaths, ageing, disease checks and egg progression occur.

If no adventurers return alive for 4 consecutive seasons, the round ends successfully: the map has become feared by humans and the treasure there is regarded as safe.

If the remaining treasure value after food expenditure is zero, the campaign is lost.

Surviving owned monsters carry forward to later rounds.

4. Treasure
There are five treasure types:
- Bronze coins: 10 credits each
- Silver coins: 30 credits each
- Gold coins: 100 credits each
- Rubies: 200 credits each
- Emeralds: 350 credits each

Treasure types differ by value. Higher-value treasure attracts stronger adventurers.

Each adventurer has a fixed carrying capacity for each treasure class. All adventurers prefer higher-value treasure over lower-value treasure, subject to what they can reach and carry.

5. Adventurer Classes
There are five adventurer classes.

5.1 Villagers
- Always possible.
- Unarmed by default.
- Low intelligence.
- Poor combat skill.
- Can pick up environmental rocks and sticks as improvised weapons.
- Easily deterred by casualties and failed raids.
- More likely if the treasure site is close to a human settlement.
- Prefer valuable treasure but may behave crudely and opportunistically.

5.2 Explorers
- Begin appearing when silver coins are present.
- More common once gold or better treasure exists.
- More intelligent than villagers.
- Wear leather armour.
- Armed with daggers.
- Better at route choice and avoiding obvious danger.
- Moderately deterred by casualties and recent failures.

5.3 Warriors
- Begin appearing when gold coins are present.
- More common once rubies or better treasure exists.
- Skilled close-combat fighters.
- Wear chain-mail armour.
- Armed with broad swords.
- Less deterred by casualties than lower classes.
- Likely to engage monsters blocking access to valuable treasure.

5.4 Knights
- Begin appearing when gems are present.
- Appear in small numbers when rubies are the best treasure.
- Appear in much greater numbers when emeralds are present.
- Mounted when terrain permits.
- Wear plate armour.
- Armed with long swords and lances.
- Dangerous in open ground.
- Can dismount in unsuitable terrain.
- Dismounted knights should still be at least as effective as warriors.
- Much less deterred by previous casualties or failures.
- Not deterred by lower-class adventurers failing to return alive.

6. Adventurer Generation
Each round has a procedurally generated context including:
- treasure distribution;
- terrain;
- distance to nearest human settlement;
- environmental availability of rocks/sticks;
- hiding places/ambush terrain;
- movement difficulty.

Adventurer numbers depend on:
- highest treasure tier present;
- total treasure value;
- distance to settlement;
- casualties and non-returning adventurers in recent rounds;
- amount of treasure successfully stolen in recent rounds;
- adventurers returning alive with treasure in recent rounds;
- class-specific attraction and deterrence.

The game remembers up to the last 4 rounds for deterrence purposes.

General rules:
- Lower-class adventurers are more numerous.
- Maximum number per class decreases as class increases.
- Villagers and explorers are strongly affected by settlement proximity and recent failures.
- Warriors are less affected.
- Knights are least affected.
- Higher treasure tiers increase the chance and number of stronger adventurers.
- Adventurers who return alive with treasure increase future adventurer pressure by proving that the site can be robbed.
- Adventurers who fail to return alive reduce future adventurer pressure by making the site feared.
- Deterrence is class-aware: each class is principally deterred by failures of its own or higher classes. Knights are not deterred by lower-class adventurers failing to return alive.

Example progression:
- Bronze only: mostly villagers.
- Bronze + silver: villagers plus some explorers.
- Gold present: more explorers, some warriors.
- Rubies present: warriors common, a few knights.
- Emeralds present: large mixed raids including many strong adventurers.

Late rounds should remain variable. For example, a later round may contain only bronze and silver but be very close to a village, causing a large villager rush in open, rock-filled terrain.

7. Adventurer Individual Variation
Each adventurer has randomised scalar traits in addition to class defaults.

Possible traits:
- bravery;
- greed;
- intelligence;
- combat skill;
- pain tolerance;
- speed;
- strength;
- perception;
- caution;
- likelihood of fleeing;
- preference for high-value treasure;
- willingness to fight monsters;
- ability to use terrain.

Class determines baseline ranges. Individual variation modifies behaviour within those ranges.

8. Monster Population
All monsters belong to one interbreeding population. They are not separate species, because interbreeding is central to gameplay.

Monsters vary procedurally by:
- body size;
- body shape;
- limbs;
- mouth/claws/horns/spines/armour;
- colour;
- patterning;
- senses;
- movement;
- temperament;
- aggression;
- fearfulness;
- intelligence;
- territoriality;
- terrain affinity;
- fertility;
- lifespan;
- disease resistance;
- injury resistance.

Owned monsters are visually marked with collars. Wild monsters are not collared.

9. Monster Control Philosophy
Monsters are autonomous. The player should not issue detailed combat orders.

During the raid phase, the player may only:
- release reserve monsters;
- recall owned monsters to base;
- capture wild monsters by clicking them.

Released monsters choose targets and behaviours based on:
- inherited behavioural traits;
- sensory capabilities;
- nearby adventurers;
- nearby treasure;
- own injuries/fear;
- terrain;
- threat level;
- hunger and territorial aggression;
- immediate combat circumstances.

The game should reward breeding appropriate behaviour rather than micromanagement.

10. Genetics
The genetic model should be complex enough to create emergent qualities, but simpler than a fully realistic biological simulation.

There should not be simple genes for fast or strong. Instead, genes influence underlying components that combine into emergent capabilities.

Example gene categories:
- limb length genes;
- muscle type genes;
- body mass genes;
- joint robustness genes;
- metabolic genes;
- sensory genes;
- reaction-time genes;
- aggression genes;
- fear genes;
- pain tolerance genes;
- armour/skin genes;
- claw/horn/jaw genes;
- fertility genes;
- lifespan genes;
- disease-resistance genes;
- pattern/colour genes.

Derived traits are calculated from multiple genes.

Examples:
- speed emerges from limb length, stride efficiency, mass, muscle, stamina and terrain affinity;
- combat power emerges from size, strength, weapons, aggression, skill-like instincts and target choice;
- survivability emerges from armour, toughness, pain tolerance, speed, fear response and healing;
- knight-killing ability may require strength, armour penetration, courage and survival under heavy damage;
- villager interception may require speed, perception and target prioritisation.

10.1 Emergent and Indirect Trait Simulation
Genes should not encode final gameplay roles, explicit tactics, fixed prey preferences, or direct top-level traits. A monster should not have a gene for being a deer hunter, a knight killer, a coward, a guardian, or a sprinter. Those outcomes should emerge from the interaction of lower-level heritable mechanisms with the current environment.

The preferred design chain is:
- allele;
- expressed phenotype or physiological/regulatory effect;
- subsystem response;
- observable trait or behavioural outcome.

For example, a monster's tendency to attack a dangerous animal should emerge from size, injury, hunger, perception, hormones/regulators, threat assessment, prior fatigue, terrain and the animal's own size/aggression, not from a species-specific attack preference. A monster's effectiveness against humans should emerge from morphology, senses, movement, behaviour, injury state, terrain and the adventurer's class/equipment, not from a human-killer gene.

Where gene category names in this document refer to apparent traits such as aggression, fear, speed, disease resistance or fertility, these are shorthand for underlying mechanisms. Implementation should express those genes through simulated biological or fantastical biological systems such as morphology, metabolism, sensory capability, hormonal regulation, immune response, reproductive physiology and age-dependent development.

Emergent behaviour is especially important. Monsters must act autonomously according to what they can perceive and their current internal state. They should not receive hidden knowledge, scripted roles, or direct target preferences that bypass perception, morphology, hunger, injury, fatigue, fear and aggression modelling.

10.2 Gene Tradeoff Principle
With the exception of rare, strongly recessive harmful alleles and deliberately minor aesthetic variation, every gene should have live tradeoffs. There should be very few universally good alleles. A gene that improves one performance dimension should normally impose a cost, vulnerability, opportunity cost, context dependence or interaction risk elsewhere.

Examples:
- increased muscle may improve damage and intimidation but raise food demand, heat stress, injury risk or growth cost;
- longer limbs may improve speed but reduce stability, turning, durability or performance in dense terrain;
- stronger aggression-regulating hormones may improve interception and dangerous-prey hunting but increase injury, exhaustion and poor retreat decisions;
- stronger fear/risk response may improve survival but allow adventurers or prey to escape;
- high reactivity may improve response time but waste energy and produce false positives;
- heavy egg investment may improve hatchling quality but reduce clutch size and parental recovery;
- strong immune clearance may reduce disease severity but increase fever, food use, inflammatory damage or fertility penalties.

Tradeoffs should be hooked into the dry-run natural selection simulator wherever the trait can plausibly affect survival, feeding, reproduction, injury, disease, terrain performance or treasure defence. Natural selection should score whole expressed genotypes in environmental context rather than selecting isolated desirable-looking numbers.

The goal is for the player to breed situationally adapted bloodlines rather than simply maximising a list of stats. Different biomes, prey profiles, adventurer threats, terrain layouts, food availability and breeding strategies should favour different combinations of alleles.

11. Inbreeding
Inbreeding must create problems.

The game should track relatedness/inbreeding sufficiently to create:
- increased chance of harmful recessive traits;
- fertility penalties;
- disease susceptibility;
- developmental weakness if desired;
- shorter lifespan;
- behavioural instability;
- reduced clutch viability.

Inbreeding can still be useful for fixing desirable traits, but should create long-term risk.

Wild monsters provide new alleles and reduce inbreeding, but may introduce undesirable genes as well as useful ones.

12. Aesthetic Genetics
Some genes are purely or mainly aesthetic.

Possible visual traits:
- purple, green, blue, cyan, red, black, white, etc.;
- stripes;
- spots;
- mottling;
- gradients;
- glowing markings;
- horn colour;
- eye colour;
- scale/skin/fur texture.

Alleles may interact. For example, if a purple allele and cyan allele have equal ranking, the monster may show patterned or mixed colouring rather than one fully dominating.

Visual appearance should be procedurally generated and consistent with genotype.

13. Procedural Monster Graphics
Graphics should be generated as simplified but expressive procedural SVG or canvas-drawn component art.

Monster appearance should reflect:
- genome;
- age;
- injury;
- disease/health;
- ownership collar;
- possibly fear/aggression state.

Monster rendering should use phenotype data from authoritative simulation snapshots. Rendering code may cache derived visual data, but visual caches must not be authoritative gameplay state.

Graphics should prioritise clarity and charm over realism. Monsters should look distinct enough that players can remember bloodlines.

The visual direction is a readable dog/crocodile-like monster silhouette with snout, tail, legs, teeth, horns/spines and collar. This phenotype style supports clarity while allowing genetic visual variety.

14. Reproduction
Monsters reproduce by laying eggs.

Reasons:
- visually clear;
- cute;
- easier to communicate;
- supports clutch mechanics;
- avoids mammalian pregnancy complexity.

Possible model:
- breeding takes the mother out of action for one turn;
- eggs hatch after a short number of turns;
- clutch size depends on genetics and age;
- offspring inherit genes from both parents;
- mutation may occur at low rates;
- hatchlings may need time to mature before breeding or effective combat.

Monsters can only breed below a certain age.

15. Ageing, Injury and Disease
Monsters can die from:
- battle injury;
- disease;
- old age.

Disease is simplified. There is no transmissible disease model.

Disease risk is based on:
- genes;
- age;
- random chance;
- injury status.

Injuries come mainly from battle. Injuries may:
- heal;
- leave permanent penalties;
- increase disease risk;
- reduce fertility;
- reduce combat effectiveness;
- cause death.

16. Wild Monsters
Wild monsters may appear randomly during raid phases.

Wild monsters:
- behave like any other monster according to genes and circumstances;
- are not harder to command because there is no command/domestication system;
- can be captured by clicking during the battle phase;
- continue acting normally for the current round;
- return to base at the end of the round if captured and alive.

Purpose:
- broaden the gene pool;
- reduce inbreeding;
- introduce rare traits;
- introduce both desirable and undesirable genes.

17. Combat
Combat is real-time and autonomous.

When a monster and adventurer engage, possible outcomes include:
- one wounds the other;
- one flees;
- one dies;
- both disengage;
- combat continues.

Combat depends on adventurer factors:
- class;
- equipment;
- armour;
- weapon;
- individual bravery;
- individual skill;
- current treasure load;
- terrain;
- mounted/dismounted status for knights.

Combat depends on monster factors:
- size;
- speed;
- attack anatomy;
- armour/toughness;
- aggression;
- fearfulness;
- intelligence;
- injury;
- terrain affinity;
- pack/cooperation traits.

Monsters should not always fight to the death. Retreat/flee behaviour should be genetically influenced.

Adventurers may keep running, fight, or flee depending on class and individual traits.

18. Terrain, Environment and Navigation
Each round has a procedurally generated landscape. The landscape is stable for that round.

Terrain affects:
- movement speed;
- hiding/ambush;
- adventurer pathfinding;
- mounted knight effectiveness;
- availability of rocks/sticks;
- monster terrain advantages;
- line of sight.

Terrain/feature categories include:
- open grassland;
- rocky ground;
- forest;
- marsh;
- stream/lake water barriers;
- tundra/ice/desert variants if useful;
- ruins/caves/narrow passes/hills.

Pathfinding and movement are part of agent behaviour, not a detached god service. Agents own movement, pathfinding and visibility decisions. Shared terrain rules interpret world facts for movement cost, traversability and visibility.

Navigation has two layers:
- coarse A* pathfinding through navigation cells;
- continuous local steering around circular obstacle footprints.

Static scenery may have separate visual radius and blocking radius. Trees, for example, may have large visual canopies but small trunk-sized blocking radii. This avoids making tree canopies artificially impassable.

Agents must validate that the segment to their next waypoint is clear before following it. If a waypoint becomes locally unusable, the agent clears the path and repaths. Local steering should use a small fan of angular detours rather than only X/Y axis sliding so agents do not wedge against circular obstacle shoulders.

Different rounds should reward different monster lines.

19. Treasure Defence and Scoring
Adventurers attempt to:
- enter the map;
- choose treasure;
- run to it;
- collect as much as they can carry;
- escape.

The player scores based on the value of remaining treasure at the end of the round.

If no adventurers return alive for four consecutive seasons, humans abandon the attempt because the site has become feared, and the round ends successfully.

Food expenditure is deducted from the end-of-round treasure value used for scoring and win/loss. Food expenditure does not remove units from physical treasure piles during the round, because allocating cost across specific piles and fractional treasure units would create unnecessary accounting and balance complexity.

The campaign loss condition is: remaining treasure value after food expenditure is zero.

20. Round Variation and Campaign Progression
Rounds should generally escalate but remain unpredictable.

Expected broad progression:
- early rounds: low treasure, villagers, simple terrain;
- mid rounds: silver/gold, explorers/warriors, stronger terrain variation;
- later rounds: gems, knights, mixed raids, more difficult layouts.

However, later rounds can still vary dramatically. Examples:
- low-value treasure near a village, causing villager swarms;
- gem-rich map far from settlement, causing fewer but elite adventurers;
- open terrain favouring knights;
- dense forest favouring ambush monsters;
- rocky terrain arming villagers with improvised weapons;
- scattered treasure requiring fast interceptors;
- concentrated treasure requiring tough guardians.

21. Import/Export
Monsters can be exported and imported.

Export should serialise core monster data:
- genome;
- age;
- sex/reproductive role if applicable;
- injury state;
- disease state;
- fertility state;
- pedigree summary if desired;
- visual cache if useful, but not authoritative.

Imported monsters in campaign mode should preferably appear through an in-game opportunity, such as:
- wandering monster dealer;
- strange wild monster;
- mysterious egg.

Sandbox mode may allow direct insertion.

Imported monsters must not require timeline contextualisation as in a historical game.

22. Design Priorities
Highest priorities:
- Artificial selection as the main gameplay.
- Autonomous monsters whose behaviour is genetically influenced.
- Clear feedback from raids.
- Procedural variation in landscapes and threats.
- Inbreeding pressure requiring occasional wild genes.
- SVG/canvas monsters that visibly reflect genetics.
- Simple but meaningful combat.
- Limited player control during raids.
- Replayability through random treasure/terrain/adventurer contexts.
- AI-debuggable architecture and deterministic headless tests.

23. Deliberately Out of Scope
To keep the project feasible, avoid:
- detailed RTS controls;
- training/domestication systems;
- transmissible disease;
- separate monster species;
- human economy simulation;
- base-building complexity;
- weather systems;
- detailed equipment inventories;
- complex nutrition;
- 3D graphics;
- multiplayer;
- large numbers of adventurer classes;
- too many treasure types.

24. Technical Framing and Architecture
The game should be architecture-first, modular and easy to debug with AI assistance.

Architecture framing:
- The project remains a single HTML file for collaboration convenience, but internally separates worker simulation from main-thread rendering.
- The authoritative simulation runs in a Web Worker.
- The main browser thread owns UI, input, canvas rendering, interpolation and event log presentation only.
- Worker snapshots are authoritative. Interpolated render snapshots are visual-only and must never be sent back into the simulation.
- GameSession is a high-level coordinator, not a god object.
- World owns persistent physical place data.
- GameState owns persistent player-owned monsters/eggs and optional campaign statistics.
- Raid owns temporary live raid state.
- Agent owns pathfinding, movement and visibility.

- Pure Traditional OO Rule
The simulation must use pure traditional object-oriented modelling. Classes should represent actual classes of things in the game world or durable conceptual entities in the simulation domain, not merely bundles of data or functions. Never create a separate class just to perform a function if that separate class does not represent an actual separate class of things in the world being simulated. If a class would be instantiated with exactly one object and that object would always belong to exactly one parent object, that is not valid OO design for this project: its fields and behaviour should instead belong to the parent class. For example, a monster's body, nutrition state, activity history and thermal condition are parts or states of the monster, so they should be fields and behaviours of Monster unless they represent independently existing world objects. Shared rules may exist as ordinary methods on the relevant domain classes, but should not be turned into detached service classes or one-child component classes.
- TerrainRules interpret terrain facts for movement and sight; they do not own world state.

Snapshot protocol:
- Static scenery/world data should not be sent every tick.
- The worker sends WORLD_SNAPSHOT after world creation.
- Repeated RAID_SNAPSHOT messages contain dynamic raid state only: agents, raid status, treasure amounts and dropped treasure.
- The main thread stores the latest static world snapshot and attaches it locally for rendering.

Logical module boundaries:
- genetics
- monsterLifecycle
- monsterPhenotype
- monsterBehaviour
- eggAndBreeding
- inbreeding
- diseaseAndInjury
- adventurers
- treasure
- terrain
- terrainRules
- agentNavigation
- combat
- roundGeneration
- raidSimulation
- snapshotProtocol
- importExport
- svgOrCanvasRendering
- saveLoad

The simulation core should remain separable from the UI to allow automated balance testing.

Module discipline:
- Nutrition, treasure, campaign scoring and win/loss rules are authoritative simulation state. The browser UI displays snapshots only.
- Rendering may cache derived visual data, but render caches must never become authoritative gameplay state.
- GameState owns persistent owned monsters, eggs and campaign statistics.
- Raid owns temporary live raid agents, dropped treasure and live raid events.
- World owns terrain, scenery, treasure deposits and round food expenditure.
- Save/load must serialise authoritative state, not canvas/interpolation state.

25. Pathfinding and Performance Policy
Pathfinding should remain single-threaded until profiling proves otherwise.

The pathfinding system uses:
- disciplined repath throttling;
- path intent keys that do not include the agent's current start cell;
- emergency repathing only when the target/intent changes materially, the path is absent, the next waypoint is invalid or the agent is genuinely stuck;
- local steering around obstacle shoulders;
- deterministic stress tests checking both monsters and adventurers for stuck windows;
- snapshot-size tests to ensure static world data is not resent every tick.  

Any multithreaded pathfinding design requires plain-data path requests, stale-result handling, priorities and deterministic application on simulation ticks.

26. Automated Balance and Regression Testing Goals
The game should support headless simulation tests.

Balance metrics to record:
- treasure lost per round;
- adventurer deaths by class;
- monster deaths by cause;
- monster injury rates;
- breeding success;
- inbreeding level;
- wild monster capture frequency;
- gene diversity;
- average monster age;
- frequency of campaign loss;
- usefulness of different monster trait clusters;
- balance between treasure values and adventurer pressure.

Architecture/regression metrics to record:
- pathfinding calls per active agent;
- stuck-window detections for monsters and adventurers;
- average and maximum simulation tick time;
- dynamic snapshot size;
- static snapshot size;
- number of static world snapshots per new world;
- number of recurring dynamic raid snapshots;
- rendering interpolation correctness.

The goal is not perfect balance, but avoiding dominant strategies and ensuring different monster builds are useful in different contexts.


26A. Campaign and Treasure Statistics
The game should support clear player-facing campaign statistics.

The statistics panel should always show whether the player is currently in a campaign or a single round game.

Treasure statistics to record and show:
- treasure at the start of the current round, listed separately for bronze coins, silver coins, gold coins, rubies and emeralds;
- for each treasure type, both the number of units and the value;
- total starting treasure units and total starting treasure value;
- treasure stolen in the current round, again by type, units and value, with totals;
- treasure remaining in the current round, again by type, units and value, with totals;
- in campaign mode, the ending treasure value after food expenditure for each preceding round played;
- at the end of each round or single-round game, an end-of-round treasure statistics screen showing win/loss state;
- the most prominent value on the end-of-round statistics screen: remaining treasure value after food expenditure.

The goal is to give the player clear feedback about what kinds of treasure are being lost or defended, without cluttering the campaign UI with internal technical metrics or averages that do not help decision-making.

27. One-Sentence Pitch
Breed a lineage of autonomous, genetically varied monsters to defend treasure from increasingly dangerous adventurers across procedurally generated raids, using wild monsters to refresh the bloodline before inbreeding, age, injury and greed destroy the hoard.

28. Nutrition and Feeding
Design intent:
- Each monster has a per-season nutrition requirement.
- The default nutrition requirement is 10 food per monster per season.
- Genetics, age, size, injury, activity level, metabolism and pregnancy/egg-laying may modify this requirement.
- Monsters can satisfy nutrition during raids by eating killed adventurers or macrofauna.
- A human normally provides 1 food.
- Macrofauna provide food according to species and size; larger macrofauna such as deer or seals should provide about 10 food.
- Eating during a raid takes time. Eating duration is modified by genetics, mouth anatomy, size, hunger and temperament.
- A fully nourished monster normally does not stop to eat a kill, but this is genetically modifiable so that greedy, compulsive or low-self-control monsters may still feed.
- A monster that is held in reserve during a raid, or that does not meet its nutrition requirement during the raid, must be fed between seasons at the player's expense if enough treasure remains.
- Feeding is automatic rather than a separate shop/micromanagement screen.
- Food cost varies by biome/environment. The default cost is 1 credit per food.
- Treasure values are measured in credits: bronze coin = 10 credits, silver coin = 30 credits, gold coin = 100 credits, ruby = 200 credits, emerald = 350 credits.
- The game keeps a running total of food expenditure and shows it in the UI.
- Food expenditure is deducted from final remaining-treasure scoring and from the win/loss value.
- Food expenditure does not remove units from physical treasure piles during the round.
- Automatic feeding must not reduce the remaining treasure value after food expenditure below zero.
- If the player cannot or does not fully feed a monster, nutrition debt creates health states.

Health states:
- Nourished: full requirement met.
- Malnourished: some food shortage; mild health, speed, fertility and disease-risk penalties.
- Severely malnourished: major penalties and high disease/death risk.
- Starved: monster dies.

Architecture notes:
- Long-term nutrition state belongs to persistent monster data in GameState.
- Raid-time feeding/eating behaviour belongs to Monster/Raid behaviour and must be reflected in authoritative worker snapshots.
- Food value should be data on edible corpses or carcasses, not hard-coded into the rendering layer.
- Main-thread UI may display nutrition, cost and food expenditure, but must not calculate authoritative feeding results.
- Nutrition should be deterministic under the seeded simulation so that headless balance tests can include food cost, malnutrition, starvation, and campaign-loss pressure.
